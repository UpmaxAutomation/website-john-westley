# John Westley — LinkedIn Outreach Sequences

---

## Outreach Principles

- Never pitch in a connection request — lead with observation or relevance
- First follow-up is value, not ask — share a specific insight, not a feature list
- Second follow-up is a soft offer — a question, not a pitch
- Hard offer (book a call) comes only after signal of interest
- Max 3 touchpoints per sequence unless they engage
- Reference specific things about their company, role, or content — never generic

---

## Sequence 1 — Plateauing CEO (cold, no mutual connection)

### Connection Request (300 char max)
```
Hi [Name], saw your company has been growing steadily — congrats. I work specifically on the structural reasons B2B revenue growth stalls or slows at your stage. Thought it was worth connecting given what you're building.
```

### Follow-Up 1 (send 3 days after connect, if no reply)
```
Hi [Name],

Thanks for connecting.

One thing I've noticed at companies in the $5M–$15M range: the revenue motion that worked to get there often creates the ceiling that prevents the next level.

Not a people issue. Usually it's one or two structural gaps — in the handoffs between teams, the retention motion, or how the conversion architecture is designed — that silently tax every dollar of new growth.

I wrote a short piece on the six most common ones if it's useful: [link to the-problem page]

Happy to share what I've seen at companies in a similar position if it would be helpful to compare notes.
```

### Follow-Up 2 (send 5 days later, if no reply)
```
[Name],

Last message from me.

Quick question: if you could diagnose one thing in your revenue motion right now — the thing you suspect is the real drag — what would you point to?

I ask because the answer usually reveals whether the problem is structural or execution. And the fix for each is completely different.

If you ever want to think it through with someone who's been in the CRO seat, happy to spend 45 minutes.

No prep, no pitch — just a diagnostic conversation.
```

## Sequence 2 — New CRO (they just started a new role)

### Connection Request
```
Hi [Name], congratulations on the new CRO role. Walking into an inherited revenue motion is one of the harder diagnostics — curious what you're finding in the first 90 days. Would be good to connect.
```

### Follow-Up 1 (3 days after connect)
```
[Name],

A pattern I've seen with CROs walking into an existing motion: the first instinct is to change the team. The more durable approach — and the one that builds credibility faster — is to diagnose the structure first.

The structure tells you which team decisions are actually necessary, versus which ones would just shuffle the same structural problem onto different people.

I spent years in CRO and CEO seats before doing this diagnostically. Happy to share what I've learned about sequencing these early decisions if it's useful.

What's the one thing about the current motion you're most uncertain about?
```

### Follow-Up 2 (5 days later)
```
[Name],

Not trying to be persistent — just one more thought.

The first 6 months in a new CRO seat are when the diagnostic window is most open. The CEO is watching. The team is in a holding pattern. The board is waiting.

A structural diagnosis completed in weeks 4–10 gives you a credible, sequenced plan to bring to the board — rather than "here's what I'm changing and why."

If you'd find value in a structured conversation about what you're walking into, I'm happy to make the time.
```

```

### Follow-Up 1 (3 days after connect)
```
[Name],

A few things that tend to be true in PE-backed revenue situations:

1. The growth model assumed faster revenue ramp than materialized
2. The operating partner says "fix sales" but the diagnosis isn't more specific than that
3. The team is working hard, but something structural is creating drag that more effort won't fix

If any of those resonate, the fastest path to a board-ready answer isn't hiring a new VP Sales. It's a structured 5-week audit that tells you exactly which structural gaps to fix, in what order.

I've done this for companies in your situation. Happy to walk you through the process if useful.
```

### Follow-Up 2 (5 days later)
```
[Name],

One question: when your board or operating partner asks about the revenue plan, is the answer clear and confident — or is it still partly "we're figuring it out"?

If it's the latter, a structured diagnosis is faster than most people think. Five weeks for the audit and diagnosis. Clear deliverables. A sequenced fix the board can follow.

If the timing is right, let's talk. No prep needed.
```

What you described in your post — [paraphrase their specific challenge] — is one of six structural patterns I've mapped across B2B companies at your stage.

Not trying to pitch — just thought the reframe might be useful given what you shared.

What's your current thinking on where the problem sits?

If you're still working through this, one offer: let's spend 45 minutes doing a structured diagnostic on your current revenue motion.

I'll ask specific questions across the full journey — marketing to sales handoff, conversion architecture, retention motion, expansion path — and you'll leave knowing exactly which structural problem to fix first.

Free, no pitch, no prep. Just a useful conversation.

Book here if useful: [link]
```

## Sequence 5 — Event / Conference Follow-Up

*Use within 48 hours of meeting someone at Pavilion, SaaStr, or similar event*

### Connection Request
```
[Name], great to meet you at [event]. The conversation about [specific thing you discussed] was exactly the kind of thing I don't get enough of. Let's stay connected.
```

### Follow-Up 1 (24 hours after connect)
```
[Name],

Following up from [event].

What you mentioned about [their specific problem] — that's almost always a structural issue in my experience. Not execution drag. Structural drag.

The quick test: if you put a different team on the same motion, would you get a different result? If the answer is "probably not," the structure needs to change, not the people.

If you want to think it through, happy to spend 45 minutes. That's usually enough to identify where the structural problem actually lives.
```
