## Case Study 1 — The Expansion Gap

**The $3.5M That Was Already There**

---

### The Situation

A B2B SaaS company at $18M ARR had a problem most CEOs would envy: their customers loved the product.

NPS was strong. Customer conversations were positive. Churn was low. And yet — ARR growth was stagnant. The company kept projecting 30–40% growth and delivering 8–12%.

The CEO's theory: the sales team needed to be stronger. The VP Sales had been in seat for 18 months. A board conversation was beginning about whether he was the right person for the next stage.

The operating hypothesis going into the engagement: personnel problem.

---

### The Diagnosis

The Revenue Journey Audit told a different story.

Acquisition was working — the marketing motion was generating qualified interest and the sales team was converting at an acceptable rate. The onboarding motion was functional. Customers were activating and staying.

But in week two of the audit, a pattern emerged.

The company had 200 active customers. Most had been customers for 2+ years. Most had exactly one product in their stack — the one they signed up for at the beginning.

There were three adjacent products that were natural fits for at least 60% of the customer base — products the company had been selling to new logos for 12 months.

Nobody was selling them to existing customers.

Not because the team wasn't capable. Not because customers didn't want them. Because the expansion motion had never been designed.

There was no trigger — no event in the customer journey that prompted an expansion conversation. There was no defined owner — CS managed relationships, AEs hunted new logos, and the gap between them was nobody's job. There was no sequence — no designed outreach, no conversation guide, no offer logic for expanding an existing relationship. There was no incentive alignment — CS was compensated on retention, not expansion; AEs were compensated on new ARR, not existing accounts.

The revenue was already there. The motion to collect it wasn't.

---

### The Intervention

Phase 3 of the engagement focused on designing and implementing the expansion motion from scratch.

Specifically:
- Defined three expansion trigger points in the customer lifecycle (milestone achievements, usage patterns, contract anniversaries)
- Created a joint CS/AE ownership model for expansion accounts, with clear handoff criteria
- Built the expansion conversation guide and offer logic for each of the three adjacent products
- Restructured CS compensation to include an expansion component
- Built the measurement framework: expansion pipeline, expansion close rate, time-to-first-expansion conversation

Implementation took approximately 10 weeks.

---

### The Result

In the six months following implementation:

**$3.5M in expansion ARR recovered** — from the existing customer base, with no new marketing spend and no new hires.

The VP Sales was not replaced. He was the wrong diagnosis. The structural gap was the expansion motion — not the person sitting in the sales seat.

---

### The Lesson

Expansion revenue is the highest-margin revenue in most B2B businesses. It doesn't require acquisition cost. It doesn't require building trust from scratch. It doesn't require a long sales cycle.

It requires a designed motion.

Most companies at this stage don't have one. The revenue sits in the existing customer base, waiting for a trigger that never comes.

The question worth asking: what percentage of your ARR growth last year came from expansion vs. new logo? And is that split by design — or by accident?

## Case Study 2 — The Invisible Ceiling

**From $6M to $84M: Same Team, Different Motion**

---

### The Situation

A B2B company had grown to $6M ARR through founder-led energy, strong product-market fit, and a scrappy team that made things happen.

Then it stopped.

Not all at once — the company kept growing, but slowly. Quarterly targets became quarterly stretches. The CEO hired a VP Sales. Then a marketing leader. Then a RevOps manager. The team kept getting better. The number kept underwhelming.

At $6M, then $7M, then $8M, the pattern was the same: effort went up, results went sideways.

The board started asking harder questions. The CEO started blaming the team. The VP Sales started protecting himself. Everyone was working — none of it was compounding.

---

### The Diagnosis

The Revenue Journey Audit mapped something the CEO hadn't been able to see from the CEO seat:

The company's revenue motion had been designed for how the company looked at $1M — when the founder was the primary seller, deals were relationship-driven, and the team was small enough that informal coordination worked.

But at $6M+, with a full revenue team, the informal motion had become a liability:

1. **No defined ICP.** Marketing was generating leads from a broad target. Sales was qualifying against different criteria. CS was onboarding different types of customers. No one agreed on who the company was actually for.

2. **Founder-dependent conversion.** The high-value deals still required the CEO to close. When he wasn't in the room, win rates dropped significantly. The motion had never been designed to replicate what he did.

3. **No expansion motion.** (Sound familiar?) The company had 150+ customers and almost no systematic expansion. The few expansions that happened were inbound from customers asking for more.

4. **Broken handoff between marketing and sales.** Marketing was generating volume. Sales was working the top 20% and ignoring the rest. The middle of the funnel was a graveyard.

5. **No designed onboarding.** Customer activation varied wildly by rep and by CS manager. Some customers became power users. Others went quiet and churned at 12 months.

Five structural taxes running simultaneously — each invisible from any single seat.

---

### The Intervention

The engagement ran for 18 months total (multiple phases, including a mid-engagement deepening as new structural layers emerged).

Key interventions, in order:

**First fix: ICP alignment.** Brought marketing, sales, and CS into the same room with data. Identified the customers who expanded, referred, and retained at highest rates. Rebuilt targeting, lead qualification, and CS segmentation around that profile.

**Second fix: Conversion architecture.** Mapped the founder's sales motion — how he discovered need, built urgency, structured the offer. Documented it. Built it into the sales playbook. Trained the team. Win rates on qualified pipeline improved within two quarters.

**Third fix: Onboarding motion.** Designed a structured 60-day onboarding sequence with defined milestones. CS went from reactive to proactive. Time-to-value dropped. 12-month churn dropped.

**Fourth fix: Expansion motion.** Built the trigger-based expansion system (similar to Case Study 1). CS compensated on expansion, not just retention.

**Fifth fix: Handoff redesign.** Rebuilt the marketing-to-sales handoff with joint ownership, shared definitions, and a clear sequence from MQL to SQL to opportunity.

---

### The Result

Over the following years, the company scaled from **$6M to $84M ARR**.

Same team. Same market. Same product.

The variable was the motion.

NRR climbed above 110% as the retention and expansion fixes took hold. Sales efficiency improved as the conversion architecture became replicable. Marketing ROI improved as ICP alignment tightened the funnel.

The CEO no longer needed to close every deal.

---

### The Lesson

The motion that gets a company to $6M is almost always a founder-dependent, informally coordinated set of behaviors that worked because the team was small enough to make it work.

It stops working when the team grows, functions separate, and the informal coordination breaks down.

The question isn't "why is the team underperforming." It's "what motion are they trying to perform — and is it designed for where the company is now?"

## Case Study 3 — The New CRO

**Six Months, Board Credibility, and the Diagnosis No One Had Made**

---

### The Situation

Rachel was three months into a new CRO role at a $15M ARR B2B tech company.

She'd been brought in to fix a revenue motion that had stalled for two years under the previous VP Sales. She was sharp, credible, had done this before. But she was walking into a situation she'd inherited — and the clock was running.

The CEO's theory: the previous team wasn't strong enough. Rachel's job was to upgrade it.

Rachel's instinct: the problem was structural. She could feel it, but she couldn't name it precisely enough to act on.

The board was waiting to see what she'd change.

---

### The Diagnosis

Rachel brought the engagement in at week 6 — before making any significant personnel decisions.

The audit revealed a specific structural pattern:

The company's sales motion had been designed for a mid-market deal ($50K ACV) but the team was spending most of its time and energy on SMB deals ($8K–$15K ACV) because those were the ones that moved fastest.

The result: a revenue motion optimized for the wrong customer.

- Marketing was generating volume in the SMB segment, where CAC was low but LTV was lower
- Sales was closing deals quickly at small contract values because those were easier wins
- CS was onboarding and managing a large, fragmented customer base with low NRR
- The mid-market segment — where the company's product had a genuine differentiator and contracts ran 5–10x larger — was being under-resourced

The structural diagnosis: **a segment misalignment between where the motion was pointing and where the real economics lived.**

No amount of team upgrading would fix this. A better team working the same motion would just close more small deals faster.

---

### The Intervention

With a specific structural diagnosis in hand, Rachel was able to:

1. Present a board-ready finding: the motion is pointed at the wrong segment. Here's the data. Here's why this is structural, not personnel.

2. Make the personnel decisions that were actually necessary — and skip the ones that weren't. One CS leader wasn't right for the redesign. One sales leader was. She kept the right one, replaced the wrong one, and had clear reasoning for each.

3. Design the motion redesign: ICP redefinition (mid-market, specific firmographics), marketing shift (content and channels targeting mid-market buyers), sales motion redesign (longer cycle, different discovery, different value architecture), CS motion redesign (higher-touch, success plans).

4. Execute with a clear sequenced plan she could present to the board at months 3, 6, and 12.

---

### The Result

By month 6:
- Mid-market pipeline had increased by 3x from a standing start
- ACV on new business had increased from $12K to $34K average
- The team restructure had been completed with minimal disruption
- The board was aligned on the diagnosis and the sequenced fix

By month 12:
- ARR was growing at the highest rate in 3 years
- NRR had improved as the mid-market customer profile (higher engagement, more expansion opportunity) began to dominate the base

Rachel's credibility with the CEO and board was established early — not from making fast changes, but from making the right ones in the right order.

---

### The Lesson

The most common mistake new CROs make is starting with personnel decisions before structural diagnosis.

Personnel decisions based on a structural misdiagnosis are expensive — in trust, in time, and in the organizational disruption they create.

A structural diagnosis in weeks 4–10 tells you:
- Which personnel decisions are actually necessary
- What the new motion needs to look like
- How to sequence the changes so each one builds on the last
- What to bring to the board as a plan, not a reaction

The diagnostic window at the start of a new CRO tenure is the most valuable asset in the role. Most people use it to look busy. The ones who use it to diagnose build durable credibility faster than anyone else in the room.
