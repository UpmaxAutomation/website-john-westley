## Slide 4 — The Six Structural Taxes

**Headline:**
> Six patterns that silently tax every dollar of B2B revenue.

**Six cards (grid layout):**

**1. The Broken Handoff**
Marketing delivers. Sales rejects. Nobody owns the middle. Pipeline dies where functions meet.

**2. The Retention Drain**
Customers arrive and leave. The acquisition motion works. The retention motion doesn't exist.

**3. The Invisible Ceiling**
Same ARR range, quarter after quarter. The ceiling is structural — baked into how the go-to-market was designed.

**4. The Missing Expansion Motion**
The highest-margin revenue in the business. Most companies never designed a motion to collect it.

**5. The Conversion Gap**
Qualified buyers go quiet at the same stage. Not a rep problem. A conversion architecture problem.

**6. The Misaligned Machine**
Every team is busy. None of it is compounding. Separate metrics, separate optimization, zero structural leverage.

**Footer note:**
> Most companies have 2–3 of these active. The question isn't whether you have them — it's which ones, and in what order to fix them.

## Slide 5 — Why This Happens

**Headline:**
> The motion that got you here creates the ceiling that stops you from going further.

**Body:**
Every B2B company builds its revenue motion around the constraints it had at early stage:
- A founder selling
- A small, generalist team
- Deals won on relationship and grit
- No designed process — just hustle that worked

It works. Growth happens.

Then at some point, the motion stops scaling.

Not because the people got worse. Because the motion was never redesigned for where the company is now.

**Key insight (large, highlighted):**
> The structural gap between your current motion and the motion you need is always specific. It's always fixable. And it's always invisible from any single seat.

## Slide 8 — The Process

**Headline:**
> Five weeks to clarity. Implementation through delivery.

**Timeline visual (three phases):**

**Phase 1 — Revenue Journey Audit (Weeks 1–3)**
Scope: Full diagnostic across every stage of the revenue journey — acquisition, handoff, conversion, onboarding, retention, expansion, referral.

Deliverable: Revenue Journey Map — a visual of where revenue is being created, where it's leaking, and what structural patterns are active.

**Phase 2 — Structural Diagnosis (Weeks 3–5)**
Scope: Root-cause diagnosis of each structural pattern identified in the audit. Prioritized sequence of fixes — what to address first, second, and third (and why order matters).

Deliverable: Structural Diagnosis Report — board-ready findings with sequenced interventions and projected impact.

**Phase 3 — Redesign & Rebuild (Weeks 5–16)**
Scope: Implementation of the redesigned motion — process, playbook, incentive structure, team alignment, and measurement.

Deliverable: A working revenue motion. Not a slide deck.

## Presenter Notes

### Key phrases to use live (not on slides):
- "The motion that got you here creates the ceiling that stops you from going further."
- "Structural problems don't respond to execution pressure. They just keep running."
- "The question isn't whether you have one of these patterns. The question is which one, and how long it's been running."
- "I diagnose. Most consultants prescribe. The difference is whether you fix the right thing first."

### When to pause and invite reaction:
- After Slide 4 (Six Taxes): "Which of those do you recognize in your current motion?"
- After Slide 7 (Why John): "Is this the kind of problem you've been trying to solve?"
- After Slide 10 (Results): "The $3.5M recovery — that happened in a company similar to yours in revenue range. Want me to walk through how we found it?"

### Objection handling:

**"We just hired a new VP Sales / CRO."**
→ "That's often the right first move. The question I'd want to answer: is the motion they're walking into structurally set up for them to succeed? A new hire is more valuable when the structure is right."

**"We've tried consultants before and it didn't stick."**
→ "What didn't stick — the diagnosis, or the implementation? Most engagements end at the slide deck. That's where I stay in the room."

**"Our problem is lead volume, not structure."**
→ "Maybe. Let me ask: when leads arrive, what percentage convert to pipeline? And at which stage do they stall? If the answer is consistent — same stage, same objection — that's structural. More leads into a broken conversion architecture just creates more expensive failures."
