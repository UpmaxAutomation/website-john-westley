### The Pattern

You've backed a business at $10M–$40M revenue. The investment thesis assumed revenue growth as the primary value-creation lever.

Twelve months in, growth hasn't materialized the way the model projected.

The operating partner says "fix sales." The CEO nods. A new VP Sales is hired. Six months later, the same problem.

The problem isn't the VP Sales.

It's the revenue motion.

---

### What We Find

In PE-backed companies at this stage, the revenue motion almost always has one or more structural gaps that execution pressure won't fix:

→ **The motion was built for an earlier version of the company** — designed when the founder was selling, deals were smaller, and informal coordination worked. It hasn't been redesigned for the current stage or team size.

→ **The expansion motion doesn't exist** — the highest-margin revenue in the business (existing customer expansion) is being left uncollected because no one owns a designed motion to capture it.

→ **The handoff is broken** — leads arrive and stall between marketing and sales. Neither team owns the middle.

→ **Customers are arriving but not staying** — acquisition is working, retention isn't. The onboarding motion is ad hoc, not designed.

→ **The team is misaligned** — separate metrics, separate optimization, no structural leverage across functions.

These aren't hypotheses. They're the six structural patterns that appear, in various combinations, in nearly every company we audit.

## Operating Partner Conversation Guide

*Use this when speaking with operating partners about introducing John to a portfolio company.*

---

### The three situations where John is most useful:

**1. "Revenue isn't growing fast enough and we don't know why."**
Thesis assumed X% growth. Actuals are half that. The CEO is trying things but nothing is sticking. This is the most common fit. A structural audit answers the "why" faster than most other interventions.

**2. "We're about to make a senior revenue hire and we're not sure it will hold."**
Before spending $300K+ on a VP Sales or CRO, a structural audit tells you whether the motion they'll be walking into is set up for them to succeed. If it isn't, the hire will underperform — and the diagnosis will be the same six months later.

**3. "We have a revenue leader who's struggling and the board is debating whether to replace them."**
Sometimes the revenue leader is the problem. More often, the motion is the problem and the revenue leader is being blamed. A structural diagnosis provides a neutral, rigorous answer — and often either validates keeping the person (with a redesigned motion) or makes the case for replacement much more specific.
