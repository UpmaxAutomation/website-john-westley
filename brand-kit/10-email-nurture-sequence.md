# John Westley — Email Nurture Sequences

---

## Sequence Overview

| Sequence | Trigger | Emails | Goal |
|----------|---------|--------|------|
| A — Post-Call, No Decision | Booked + completed a call, no next step | 4 emails over 30 days | Re-engage, move to proposal |
| B — Requested Info, Didn't Book | Downloaded content or replied to LinkedIn | 3 emails over 14 days | Get them to book a call |
| C — Proposal Sent, No Response | Sent proposal, silence for 5+ days | 3 emails over 10 days | Get a yes/no decision |
| D — Long-Term Nurture | Qualified prospect, not the right timing | 4 emails over 90 days | Stay relevant until timing is right |

---

## Sequence A — Post-Call, No Decision

*Use when: the diagnostic call went well, they were engaged, but no next step was confirmed or they said "let me think about it."*

---

### Email A1 — Send 24 hours after call

**Subject:** What I heard in our conversation

```
[Name],

Good conversation yesterday.

Here's what I took away from it — tell me if I'm reading it right:

[2–3 sentences summarizing the specific structural problem you identified during the call — be precise, not generic]

The pattern you described — [their specific situation] — is one I've seen before. It's structural. And the reason it persists across team changes and methodology changes is that no one has named it precisely enough to sequence the fix.

I've sent along a [case study / one-pager] that maps a similar situation. Not identical — but the structural pattern is the same and the sequence of interventions tracks closely to what I'd recommend for you.

[Link or attachment]

I'll follow up in a few days. If you have questions in the meantime, reply here.

— John
```

---

### Email A2 — Send 5 days after call

**Subject:** One thing I should have said on our call

```
[Name],

One thing I didn't say clearly enough on our call:

The diagnostic isn't about confirming my working hypothesis.

It's about finding out if I'm wrong — and if so, what's actually going on.

I had a working hypothesis about [their situation] going into our conversation. I still think it's directionally right. But two weeks with your team might reveal something different. That's the point.

What the Revenue Journey Audit produces isn't a recommendation shaped by what I expected to find. It's a map of where revenue is actually leaking — and sometimes that's different from what either of us thought.

That specificity is what makes the fix hold.

If the timing is right to move forward, reply and I'll send over a simple scope. If not, I understand — just wanted to leave that thought.

— John
```

---

### Email A3 — Send 12 days after call

**Subject:** The cost of the structural tax (a number)

```
[Name],

A quick thought on timing.

Structural taxes run continuously.

While the fix is being deferred, the tax keeps charging. The broken handoff keeps losing qualified interest. The expansion motion keeps not running. The customers who should be renewing keep deciding whether to stay without a designed reason to.

I'm not trying to pressure the decision. But it's worth knowing what the tax is costing per quarter in real terms — not a vague "this is expensive" observation, but a number.

In the audit, one of the first things I produce is a rough estimate of the revenue impact of each structural gap — what fixing it would likely recover or unlock. That number usually reframes the conversation about whether and when to move forward.

If you'd find it useful, I'm happy to do a rough estimate based on what you shared in our call. No charge, just a number to inform the decision.

Reply if that would help.

— John
```

## Sequence B — Requested Info, Didn't Book

*Use when: someone downloaded content, replied to a LinkedIn post, or reached out with a question but hasn't booked a call.*

---

```

One thing worth knowing: the [piece they downloaded / question they asked] is usually the surface of a deeper structural issue. Most of the companies I work with start with [surface symptom] and discover the actual structural problem is [underneath it].

If that's relevant to your situation, a 45-minute conversation is usually enough to tell you whether you have a structural problem and where it lives. No prep, no pitch.

You can book here: [link]

Or if there are more questions first, reply here — I'm happy to answer them.

— John Westley
Growth That Holds | growththatholds.com
```

## Sequence C — Proposal Sent, No Response

*Use when: proposal was sent, they received it, but 5+ days have passed with no reply.*

---

### Email C1 — Send 5 days after proposal

**Subject:** Quick check on the proposal

```
[Name],

Wanted to follow up on the proposal I sent [date].

One question: is there a specific part of the scope, timeline, or investment that's creating hesitation? If so, I'd rather know than guess.

Sometimes the fit is right but the timing isn't — in which case we can talk about how to sequence the start. Sometimes the scope needs adjustment. Either way, I'd find it useful to know where you are.

Reply here when you have a minute.

— John
```

---

### Email C2 — Send 8 days after proposal

**Subject:** The decision I'd suggest making before this one

```
[Name],

Before deciding whether to move forward with this engagement, there's a question worth answering first:

Is the problem you're trying to solve structural or execution?

If it's execution — if the team knows what to do and isn't doing it — this engagement probably isn't the right fit. A manager or a coach would be more useful.

If it's structural — if the pattern keeps repeating regardless of who's in the role or what methodology is running — then execution fixes will keep disappointing you, and the structural problem will keep compounding.

Our call pointed to [specific structural signal from their call]. That's structural.

If I got that wrong — let me know. But if it resonates, the decision to delay this engagement has a cost that's worth naming.

— John
```

---

### Email C3 — Send 12 days after proposal

**Subject:** Yes or no — either is fine

```
[Name],

I want to make this easy.

If you'd like to move forward, reply and we'll confirm a start date. I'll send the engagement agreement.

If the timing isn't right or this isn't the right fit, just reply and let me know. No hard feelings — I'd rather know than wonder.

Either way, I appreciate you taking the time to talk through what's going on at [Company].

— John
```

## Email Writing Rules (for all sequences)

- Subject lines: specific, not clever. Reference the actual topic.
- Never open with "I hope this finds you well" or "Just following up"
- Every email delivers something useful — an insight, a reframe, a specific question
- Keep emails under 250 words. If it needs to be longer, it's a proposal, not a nurture email.
- One CTA per email. Either book a call, reply with an answer, or "let me know"
- Never attach more than one thing per email
- Sign with first name only after first email
- Never use a P.S. that's longer than the email
