### Post 1 — The Six Structural Taxes (Hook Post)

```
Most B2B revenue problems aren't people problems.

They're structural ones.

I've held the CRO, VP Sales, VP Marketing, and CEO seats.

In every company, in every role, across every revenue range — the same six patterns keep showing up.

I call them Structural Taxes. They run quietly in the background, taxing every dollar of growth regardless of how hard the team works.

Here's what they look like:

1. The Broken Handoff
Marketing delivers leads. Sales says they're bad. Marketing says sales can't close.
Both are wrong. The handoff architecture is broken. Nobody owns the in-between.

2. The Retention Drain
Customers arrive. Customers leave. Acquisition costs keep climbing.
Not a CS problem. A motion problem. The onboarding and adoption sequence was never designed to hold.

3. The Invisible Ceiling
You hit $8M. Then $8M again. Then $7.8M.
It's not the market. It's not the team. It's a ceiling baked into the go-to-market design.

4. The Missing Expansion Motion
Your best-margin revenue comes from customers who buy more.
Most companies don't have a designed expansion motion. They hope for it.

5. The Conversion Gap
Qualified prospects go quiet at the same stage, over and over.
Reps get blamed. But the conversion architecture is the real culprit.

6. The Misaligned Machine
Every team is working hard.
None of it is compounding.
When teams optimize for their own metric, the system loses.

—

The frustrating thing: these taxes are invisible from any one seat.

The CRO sees pipeline.
The VP Sales sees close rates.
The CEO sees the number.

No one sees the full journey.

That's what I do.
```

### Post 2 — Contrarian (Hire Better People)

```
"We need a better VP Sales."

I hear this constantly.

Sometimes it's true.

More often — and I mean significantly more often — it's wrong.

The evidence:
→ The last VP Sales was good. They didn't hit the number.
→ The VP Sales before that was also good. Same result.
→ The team is trying hard. Pipeline looks reasonable. But conversion keeps stalling at the same stage.

Here's what that pattern tells me:

It's not a people problem. It's a motion problem.

The revenue motion is designed the way it is. And until that changes, whoever you put in the seat will underperform in the same specific ways.

Before you start the VP Sales search — diagnose the motion.

Ask:
→ Where, specifically, does qualified interest turn into stalled pipeline?
→ What happens to customers after they sign? Is there a designed onboarding motion or does it happen ad hoc?
→ When deals go quiet, is it consistent — same stage, same type of buyer, same objection?

Consistency in failure patterns is structural information.

A great VP Sales in a broken motion doesn't fix the motion.

They just fail differently.
```

### Post 3 — Story Post (The $3.5M Expansion Gap)

```
A CEO called me because his company had a revenue problem.

Pipeline was fine. Close rates were acceptable. But ARR kept stalling.

He was about to hire his third VP Sales in four years.

I spent two weeks mapping his full revenue journey — acquisition through close, onboarding, adoption, expansion, referral.

Week two, I found it.

The expansion motion didn't exist.

His company had 200 customers. Most of them had been customers for 2+ years. Most had exactly one product in their stack — the one they bought when they signed.

There were three adjacent products that were obvious fits for at least 60% of the base.

No one was selling them.

Not because the reps didn't know about them. Because no one had designed the motion for how customers moved from product one to product two.

No trigger. No handoff. No sequence. No incentive.

$3.5M in annual expansion revenue — sitting in the existing customer base, invisible.

We built the expansion motion. We incentivized CS and AE to jointly own it. We created the trigger and sequence.

Six months later: $3.5M recovered.

Same team. No new VP Sales.

The problem was never the people.
```

### Post 4 — New CRO Post

```
Walking into a new CRO role is one of the hardest diagnostics in business.

You inherit:
→ A team that may or may not be the problem
→ A motion that may or may not be broken
→ A CEO who has a theory about what's wrong
→ A board that's waiting to see what you'll do about it

The instinct is to change the team.

Fast. Visible. Decisive.

But the most common mistake I see new CROs make is exactly this: they change the people before they diagnose the structure.

And then they've spent credibility on personnel decisions — only to discover that the motion is still broken, the new team is underperforming for the same structural reasons, and now they're 6 months in with less political capital.

The faster path to credibility:

Diagnose the structure first.

A structural diagnosis done in weeks 4–10 tells you:
→ Which team decisions are actually necessary (often fewer than you think)
→ Which structural problems to fix first, second, and third
→ What the board should actually be measuring
→ How to present a sequenced plan that looks like leadership, not reaction

The best new CROs I've seen build durable credibility fast by walking in with questions — not answers.

The structure tells you which answers are right.
```

### Post 5 — PE-Backed CEO Post

```
The PE-backed revenue conversation usually goes like this:

Operating partner: "The revenue isn't where it should be. Fix sales."

CEO: "Understood."

Six months later: new VP Sales, new methodology, same problem.

The missing piece: a diagnosis.

"Fix sales" is a starting point. It's not a plan.

The actual question is: what specifically, in the revenue motion, is generating the drag?

Is it:
→ Leads arriving but not converting? (Conversion architecture problem)
→ Deals closing but customers not staying? (Onboarding and retention problem)
→ Customers happy but not expanding? (Expansion motion problem)
→ Everything fine in silos, nothing compounding? (Alignment problem)

Each one has a different fix. And fixing the wrong one first can make the others worse.

I've done this diagnostic for PE-backed companies at the $10M–$40M range.

The result the board actually wants isn't "we hired a new VP Sales."

It's a sequenced fix: here's what's structurally broken, here's the order we're fixing it, here's what you'll see by quarter.

That's a plan. Not a hope.
```

### Post 6 — The Handoff Post

```
The marketing-to-sales handoff is where most B2B pipeline goes to die.

Not because marketing is bad at generating leads.
Not because sales is bad at closing.

Because no one owns the middle.

Here's what broken looks like:

→ Marketing sends leads over when they hit a scoring threshold
→ Sales works the ones that look easy, ignores the rest
→ Marketing says close rates are a sales problem
→ Sales says lead quality is a marketing problem
→ Both are partially right

The actual problem: the handoff is a moment, not a motion.

There's no designed sequence for what happens between "marketing qualified" and "sales qualified."

No routing logic. No time-to-first-contact standard. No clear definition of when a lead is actually ready vs. being forced through a scoring system. No mechanism for marketing to learn what converted and why.

The fix isn't a new tool or a new SLA.

It's redesigning the handoff as a revenue motion — with joint ownership, shared metrics, and a sequence that converts curiosity into pipeline.

The companies that do this right stop fighting about lead quality.

They start compounding.
```

### Post 7 — Diagnosis vs. Prescription

```
Most consultants arrive with a framework.

They present it in week one.

It has a name. Sometimes a trademark. Usually a slide with arrows.

Then they spend the next three months proving that your problem fits their framework.

This is backwards.

The right order:

First, understand what's actually broken.

That requires looking at the full revenue journey — from first touch to close to expansion to referral — across every function that touches it.

It requires being willing to not know the answer in week one.

It requires having been in every seat, because the problem in one function is almost always caused by something upstream in another function.

The marketing team isn't generating bad leads. The handoff is losing them.
The reps aren't underperforming. The conversion architecture is broken.
CS isn't failing to retain. The onboarding motion doesn't set them up to succeed.

Diagnosis before prescription.

The right answer depends entirely on what's actually broken.
```

The question isn't whether you have one of these structural problems.

### Post 11 — Q1 Reflection Post (Seasonal)

```
Q1 is done.

Before you run the post-mortem, here's a question:

Is your explanation for the number this quarter the same explanation you gave last quarter?

→ "Sales execution was inconsistent"
→ "Marketing lead quality was mixed"
→ "We had some large deals push to Q2"

These are execution explanations.

They're not wrong. But if the same explanations appear every quarter, you're not looking at execution anymore.

You're looking at structure.

Structural problems generate consistent patterns.

The deals that push are always the same type.
The leads that don't convert always stall at the same stage.
The customers who churn always leave at the same point in the journey.

That consistency is information.

If you keep offering execution explanations for structural patterns, the pattern will keep repeating.

What does your Q1 pattern tell you, structurally, about the motion?
```

### Post 12 — The Real Reason They're Quiet Post

```
The prospect went quiet.

You sent three follow-ups. Silence.

The rep is blamed.

Here's what's actually happening, most of the time:

The conversion architecture is missing.

There's no designed motion for what happens after the demo. No clear next step owned by both sides. No sequence that creates momentum toward a decision.

The prospect was interested. They're still interested.

But there's no bridge from "intrigued" to "committed" — and without that bridge, silence is the default.

This isn't a sales skill problem.

The best reps in the world can't close deals when the architecture for closing is missing.

Here's the test:

→ Do deals stall consistently at the same stage?
→ Do the same objections show up at the same point?
→ Does "let me think about it" mean the deal is dead?

If yes to any of these — the conversion architecture is broken.

Not the rep.
```

## Post Formatting Rules

- No headers or bullets in the first 3 lines (LinkedIn hides them above the fold)
- First line must stop the scroll — provocative, specific, or counterintuitive
- 3–5 word max per line in the punchy opening
- Use `→` for list items (not `•` or `-`)
- One clear takeaway in the final 2–3 lines
- CTA: question or soft observation — never "Book a call" in the post itself
- Optimal length: 150–300 words for engagement, 400–600 for thought leadership
- Hashtags: 3 max at the bottom if used at all (`#B2BRevenue #SaaS #RevenueGrowth`)
